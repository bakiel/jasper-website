**Post 2: Why Your Model Failed the Audit**

3 reasons your financial model failed DFI due diligence:

1. **Circular references**
The classic Debt → Interest → Cash loop. Excel can't solve it cleanly. You get iteration errors or wrong numbers.

2. **No audit trail**
"Where did this number come from?" If you can't trace every calculation to source assumptions, you fail.

3. **Manual formula errors**
1,000+ cells. Copy-paste formulas. One mistake in row 847 that nobody catches until the funder does.

The fix isn't "be more careful."

The fix is a different methodology:
- Resolve circular references systematically
- Map every cell before building
- Validate automatically, not manually

Models should be engineered, not assembled.

### Methodology

---

**Post 4: The Single Circular Reference Rule**

Every financial model has ONE unavoidable circular reference:

Debt → Interest → Cash → Debt

Here's how most modellers handle it:
- Enable Excel iterations
- Hope it converges
- Accept "close enough"

Here's how we handle it:
- Solve algebraically before building
- Calculate exact values
- Zero circular references in the final file

The result?
- No iteration warnings
- No variance
- Passes every audit

Mathematical certainty > Excel iteration.

---

We generate them.

Why this matters:

**Post 6: Pre-Mapping: The Unsexy Secret**

The least exciting part of our process is the most important.

Before we write a single formula, we map every cell:

```
Cell        Formula              Source
─────────────────────────────────────────
C7          [Input]              User
E63         =SUM(E10:E62)        Calc
C15         =Revenue-COGS        X-ref
```

Every. Single. Cell.

It takes hours. It's tedious.

But it means:
- No surprises during build
- Every reference verified
- Audit trail from start
- Zero broken links

The boring work is what makes the output bulletproof.

### Education

---

**Post 10: What DFIs Actually Look For**

Applied to IFC/AfDB/ADB and got rejected?

Here's what they actually evaluate:

1. **Exit Strategy** — How do they get their money back?
2. **Proven Traction** — Is this theoretical or validated?
3. **Team Credibility** — Can these people execute?
4. **Financial Rigour** — Do the numbers actually work?
5. **Impact Metrics** — Jobs, ESG, development outcomes

Most applications fail on #4 and #5.

The model has errors. Or the impact story isn't quantified.

Fix these two things and your approval probability jumps significantly.

---

**Post 11: The Pilot Budget Strategy**

Pro tip for DFI applications:

Don't ask for all the money at once.

Structure your ask like this:

**Option A:** $7.5M pilot (6 months, prove concept)
**Option B:** $15M pilot (dual location, prove scale)
**Option C:** $30M asset-backed (lower risk profile)

Then: "Successful pilot → full deployment"

Why this works:
- Lower initial risk for funder
- Clear go/no-go criteria
- Demonstrates you understand their concerns
- Shows capital discipline

Funders aren't afraid of big numbers. They're afraid of unvalidated big numbers.

---

**Post 12: The Three Documents Every DFI Wants**

Before you write 100 pages, make sure you have these three:

**1. One-Pager**
Investment ask, key metrics, team snapshot.
For: Initial screening (2-minute review)

**2. Executive Summary (2-4 pages)**
Problem, solution, traction, financials, ask.
For: IC Chairperson (10-minute review)

**3. Full Business Case (30-100 pages)**
Everything in detail.
For: Full IC review (deep dive)

Most applicants only prepare #3.

Smart applicants prepare all three, in that order.

Because different people in the decision chain need different depths.
